How to Appeal a Birmingham Clean Air Zone Fine

Last updated: June 2026  ·  General information only, not legal advice  ·  ZoneAppeal is independent and not affiliated with Birmingham City Council or any government body

Birmingham's Clean Air Zone (CAZ) has been active since June 2021, covering much of the city centre. Cameras automatically check vehicle compliance as you drive through — and if your vehicle doesn't meet the required emission standard and you haven't paid the daily charge, a Penalty Charge Notice (PCN) arrives in the post.

If you've received one of these, you have two options: pay it or challenge it. This guide explains the recognised grounds for challenging a Birmingham CAZ fine, how the appeal process works, and what evidence you actually need to have a realistic chance of success.

Understanding the Birmingham CAZ

Birmingham operates a Class D Clean Air Zone — the strictest category, covering all vehicle types including cars, taxis, vans, HGVs, and buses. The zone covers the inner ring road and most of the city centre.

The daily charge for non-compliant vehicles is:

  • Cars, taxis, and light goods vehicles: £8 per day
  • Heavy goods vehicles, buses, and coaches: £50 per day

If you drive through without paying, Birmingham City Council will issue a PCN. The initial penalty is typically £120, reduced to £60 if paid within 14 days. If you ignore it, the charge escalates to £180 and can be referred to enforcement agents.

The Appeal Process: Step by Step

There are two stages before you reach an independent tribunal:

Stage 1: Informal Challenge (to Birmingham City Council)

You can challenge the PCN directly with Birmingham City Council within 28 days of the notice date (or within 28 days of a Notice to Owner if you didn't receive the original PCN). This is done online via the council's own portal. At this stage, you explain your grounds and provide any evidence. The council will either accept your challenge (cancelling the PCN), reject it, or offer a reduced settlement.

Stage 2: Formal Representation

If the council rejects your informal challenge, they'll issue a Notice of Rejection along with a Notice to Owner. You then have 28 days to make a formal representation. The council will review it again.

Stage 3: Independent Appeal (Traffic Penalty Tribunal)

If your formal representation is rejected, you can appeal to the Traffic Penalty Tribunal (TPT) — an independent body that is completely separate from the council. This is the most important stage: the TPT adjudicator is not employed by Birmingham City Council and has no interest in the outcome. Tribunal appeals are often conducted in writing, though you can request a hearing.

Important: Challenging a PCN does not automatically pause the clock on the discounted payment period. If your challenge is rejected, check whether the discount period still applies — councils vary on this point.

Recognised Grounds for Appeal

This is the most important part of this guide. Councils and the Traffic Penalty Tribunal assess appeals based on whether there is a recognised legal or factual ground — not on whether the fine feels unfair or the amount seems excessive.

The following are the grounds that most commonly succeed:

Ground Strength Evidence needed
You already paid the daily charge before the PCN was issued Strong Payment confirmation email, bank statement, transaction reference
The vehicle was sold before the contravention date Strong V5C transfer record, sale agreement, DVLA notification confirmation
The vehicle was purchased after the contravention date Strong Purchase agreement, V5C, dealer invoice
Your registration plate was cloned Strong Police report reference, photos of your vehicle showing different characteristics, evidence you were elsewhere
Administrative error on the PCN (wrong reg, wrong date, wrong vehicle class) Strong The PCN itself — the error is usually self-evident
You were directed into the zone by an official police or roadworks diversion Moderate Dashcam footage, photos of diversion signage, one.network or council roadworks reference, incident number if police involved
Zone entry signage was missing, obscured, or inadequate Moderate Dated photos or dashcam footage showing the specific approach route and signage condition
Vehicle compliance database error (vehicle incorrectly listed as non-compliant) Moderate V5C showing engine specification, manufacturer compliance confirmation letter
You didn't realise the zone was chargeable Weak Rarely succeeds on its own — only viable if combined with signage evidence
Financial hardship Weak Not a recognised ground at tribunal stage, though councils may exercise discretion informally

The Diversion Defence: Birmingham's Most Common Strong Ground

One of the most frequently cited grounds in Birmingham specifically is being directed into the zone by roadworks or a police diversion. Birmingham city centre has extensive ongoing roadworks — particularly around the Midland Metro tram extension and HS2 enabling works — which have repeatedly caused official diversions routing traffic through the CAZ.

If you were following official diversion signs and had no reasonable alternative, this is a genuine ground. The challenge is evidence: if you have dashcam footage or photographs showing the diversion signage, your case is considerably stronger than a verbal account alone.

When drafting your challenge, be specific: name the road, the date, the approximate time, and the direction you were travelling. Vague submissions are easier to reject.

Cloned Plates: What to Do

Vehicle cloning — where criminals copy a legitimate registration plate onto a different vehicle — is more common than most people realise. If your vehicle was caught by a CAZ camera but you have evidence you were elsewhere at the time, or if the vehicle in the camera image is clearly different from yours (different colour, make, model, or visible differences), this is one of the strongest grounds available.

Steps to take immediately:

  1. Report it to the police and obtain a reference number
  2. Take dated photographs of your vehicle clearly showing make, model, colour, and any distinguishing features
  3. If available, gather evidence of your whereabouts at the time of the alleged contravention
  4. Request a copy of the contravention photograph from Birmingham City Council — you're entitled to this

How to Write an Effective Challenge

Whether you're at the informal challenge stage or formal representation stage, the same principles apply:

  • State your ground clearly and early. Don't bury the reason in paragraph three. Open with it.
  • Be specific about facts. Dates, times, road names, directions of travel all matter.
  • Attach evidence. A challenge without supporting evidence is much easier to reject than one with photographs, receipts, or reference numbers attached.
  • Reference the relevant framework. For Birmingham CAZ, the relevant legislation is the Transport Act 2000 and the Road User Charging Schemes (Penalty Charges, Adjudication and Enforcement) (England) Regulations 2013.
  • Don't apologise or express regret. Keep it factual and formal.

What Happens at the Traffic Penalty Tribunal

If you reach the TPT stage, an independent adjudicator will review the evidence from both you and the council. The adjudicator can:

  • Allow your appeal — the PCN is cancelled
  • Dismiss your appeal — you must pay the full outstanding amount
  • Refer the case back to the council in certain circumstances

Most TPT appeals are decided on the papers (written submissions) without a hearing, though you can request one. Decisions are usually issued within a few weeks.

One important point: if you reach the TPT stage, the council cannot apply for costs against you simply because you lost. You won't be penalised financially for making a genuine appeal.

Common Mistakes That Weaken Appeals

  • Submitting an emotional argument rather than a factual one
  • Missing deadlines — the 28-day windows are strict
  • Not requesting the contravention photograph before drafting your challenge
  • Confusing the CAZ daily charge with a parking PCN — the legal framework is different
  • Paying before checking whether you have grounds — payment is usually treated as acceptance

Frequently Asked Questions

Does challenging a PCN stop the fine from increasing?

Making a formal challenge or representation usually freezes the penalty at its current level while the challenge is being considered. However, confirm this with Birmingham City Council when you submit — practices can vary.

Can I appeal after the deadline?

In exceptional circumstances, late appeals may be accepted — but this is at the council's discretion at the informal stage and the TPT's discretion thereafter. Don't rely on this. Act within the stated deadlines.

What if the council doesn't respond within the time limit?

If Birmingham City Council fails to respond to your formal representation within 56 days, the PCN is deemed cancelled by operation of law. Keep records of all submissions and their dates.

I've already paid — can I still appeal?

No. Payment is generally treated as acceptance of the penalty. This is one of the most important reasons to check your grounds before paying.

Does the Traffic Penalty Tribunal cost anything?

No. Appealing to the TPT is free. There are no tribunal fees for the driver.
